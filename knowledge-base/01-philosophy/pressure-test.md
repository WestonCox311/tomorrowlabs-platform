## What this document is

The strongest critique TomorrowLabs can write of its own philosophy, with honest responses. Six adversarial critiques from a composite skeptical reviewer (senior linguist, foundation officer, partner community member), followed by TomorrowLabs's honest answer to each.

The exercise serves three purposes:
1. Surface real problems in the philosophy before external critics do
2. Force operational commitments that the philosophy alone left abstract
3. Demonstrate willingness to be self-critical as a credibility signal

## The six critiques

### 01. "Glottolog as tool, not truth" is a slogan. Show me the mechanism.
**TomorrowLabs's response:** Acknowledged. The schema records disagreement but doesn't yet operationalize it. Commitment: by Q4 2026, every product displaying language metadata will have a community-override mechanism affecting what the product *does*, not just what the database records.

### 02. Your "data flywheel" extracts from communities and rewards you. Name the asymmetry.
**TomorrowLabs's response:** Acknowledged. There is no written benefit-sharing formula. Commitment: by Q2 2027, published benefit-sharing framework with specific percentages, applied per language, audited annually, enforceable through partnership agreements.

### 03. You're building a research database while pretending it's a product company.
**TomorrowLabs's response:** Partially accepted. TomorrowLabs is honestly a hybrid — commercial product success funding research-grade infrastructure. The pitch needs to include the hybrid model explicitly, including the productivity tax that comes with treating data infrastructure as research-grade.

### 04. You're claiming a multi-generational commitment from a four-person startup.
**TomorrowLabs's response:** Largely accepted. Commitment: by end of 2027, every audio corpus has a designated long-term archive partner outside TomorrowLabs's control. By Q4 2027, a legally binding sunset clause describing what happens to data assets if the organization fails.

### 05. "Confidence as first-class data" doesn't fix bias. It just labels it.
**TomorrowLabs's response:** Accepted with caveats. Two responses: (a) the data_coverage_gaps view explicitly surfaces sparseness, (b) field partnership work is the corrective mechanism for asymmetric data. Acknowledgment: until partnerships produce data, the database remains lopsided, and explicit "epistemic asymmetry warnings" should accompany comparative claims.

### 06. Where is the community in the writing of this document?
**TomorrowLabs's response:** Hardest critique. Fully accepted. The philosophy was drafted entirely by Weston working with an AI assistant, with no partner community member in the room. The document is marked provisional. Within 12 months, v2 with active co-authorship from at least one representative from each primary partner community (Cambodia, Guatemala, Mexico).

## The hardest meta-critique

> "You wrote a philosophy because it makes the work easier to fundraise, not because it changed the work."

The honest answer: the way to know whether this is real or theater is to look in 18 months at which commitments got done, which were quietly dropped, and which were renegotiated in TomorrowLabs's own favor. The pressure-test should be revisited annually with that question.
