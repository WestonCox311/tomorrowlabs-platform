TomorrowLabs · Adversarial Review
Companion to Data Philosophy v1

The strongest critique we can write of ourselves.

Six challenges from a sophisticated, skeptical reviewer. Each one taken seriously. Each one answered honestly, including where the answer is "they're right and we need to change."

Who is this critic? Imagine a composite of three readers: a senior linguist with twenty years of community partnership experience who has watched well-meaning projects flatten the communities they meant to serve; a foundation program officer who has funded language preservation for fifteen years and learned to distrust elegant pitches; and a community member from a language TomorrowLabs hopes to work with, reading the philosophy and asking whether it actually protects them.

The critique below is the document those three readers would write if asked to be brutally honest. The responses are written in TomorrowLabs's voice — sometimes pushing back, sometimes conceding, always trying to be more honest than comfortable.

01
"Glottolog as tool, not truth" is a slogan. Show me the mechanism.
The critic

You wrote a beautiful sentence about Glottolog being a tool rather than truth. I have read versions of this sentence in two dozen language preservation pitches over the years. It is almost always followed by a project that uses academic classifications as default and treats community pushback as an exception to be handled case-by-case.

What is the mechanism by which community disagreement actually changes what your product does? Not what your schema records — what your product does. If a K'iche' community partner tells you tomorrow that your classification is wrong, what specifically happens? Who has authority to override Glottolog? On what timeline? With what budget? If the answer is "we'll record their position in the community_positions table," you have not given them power, you have given them a comment box.

A schema is not a politics. A community position with no operational consequence is a footnote.

TomorrowLabs's response

This is the strongest critique in the document and it is correct. The current schema records disagreement; it does not yet operationalize it. The community_positions table is necessary but insufficient.

What we are committing to in response: by Q4 2026, every product that displays language metadata will have a community-override mechanism that affects what the product displays, not just what the database records. If a community says their language is named differently than Glottolog's preferred form, our products will use the community name, with the academic name as secondary metadata visible only to researchers. If a community says a variety is its own language, our product treats it as its own language — separate product_status row, separate visual identity, separate content track.

What we are not committing to: giving every individual claiming community authority unilateral override of academic classification. We will need governance structures for who counts as community authority in cases of internal disagreement. That governance is itself contested and we do not have a clean answer yet.

The critic is right that we wrote a sentence. The sentence needs to become a mechanism. The work to make that true is on our roadmap and we will not consider the philosophy honest until it is done.

02
Your "data flywheel" extracts from communities and rewards you. Name the asymmetry.
The critic

The platform thesis you describe — communities contribute audio recordings, recordings train models, improved models attract more communities — is structurally identical to how Big Tech accumulated language data over the past decade. The Cambodian grandmother contributes her stories. The corpus improves Khmer ASR. The improved ASR makes Babagigi more valuable. The added value accrues to TomorrowLabs's enterprise valuation and to whichever vendor's API you're calling.

You will tell me about benefit-sharing. I have read benefit-sharing language in dozens of projects. In practice it often means: communities get free access to the product they helped build, and the organization keeps the upside. That is not benefit-sharing. That is "we let you use the thing you made."

If this is genuinely different, prove it with money flow. What percentage of TomorrowLabs revenue attributable to a language goes back to that language's community? Is there a written formula? Is it enforceable? Is it audited? Or is it discretionary, which means it will be discretionary downward when there is pressure on margins?

TomorrowLabs's response

This critique cuts deeper than the philosophy currently answers. We have written about benefit-sharing as a principle. We have not yet committed to a formula.

The honest current state: TomorrowLabs is a for-profit entity with a nonprofit arm in formation. The two-entity model is partly an attempt to address exactly this asymmetry — the commercial arm pays for itself, the nonprofit arm holds community-co-owned assets. But that structure is still aspirational. As of today, there is no written formula for what percentage of Babagigi revenue from a Khmer family flows back to Khmer-language preservation work, and the critic is right to call that out.

What we will commit to by Q2 2027: a published benefit-sharing framework with specific percentages, applied per language, audited annually, enforceable through partnership agreements. We do not yet know what the right percentages are. We will not pretend we do. We will work them out with the first three community partners — Cambodia, Guatemala, Mexico — and publish what we land on, including the disagreements we had to resolve.

What we cannot commit to: that this will be easy or unanimous. Different communities will want different structures. Some will want money. Some will want infrastructure investment. Some will want governance roles. The framework needs to hold all of that without collapsing to the easiest single answer. That work is underway and the philosophy as written underspecifies it.

03
You're building a research database while pretending it's a product company.
The critic

Look at what you are actually building. Twenty-one tables. EGIDS classifications. Time-series transmission assessments. Glottolog integration. PHOIBLE cross-references. This is the data architecture of a research institute, not a consumer products company. The schema dwarfs anything Babagigi v1 will actually need.

Either (a) you are over-engineering at a stage when scrappy iteration is more important than schema purity, which will cost you product velocity, or (b) you are quietly transitioning from a startup into a research organization without being honest with your investors about what they are funding. Either way, the philosophy obscures the actual identity of the organization.

Pick. Are you Mozilla Common Voice with a product attached, or are you a consumer startup that happens to use language data? Because the philosophy reads like the former while your fundraising probably presents the latter.

TomorrowLabs's response

The critic is pointing at something real, but the framing forces a false binary. The honest answer is: we are both, and that is intentional, and the philosophy is the bridge between the two.

What we are not: a pure consumer products company that will optimize for speed-to-market and treat data infrastructure as overhead. If we were that, we would be wrong to take community partnership work seriously, and the schema would be vanity engineering. We are not that.

What we are not: a pure research institute that produces papers and datasets without commercial accountability. If we were that, Babagigi and LDL would be research demos, not products people pay for. We are not that either.

What we are: a hybrid organization that uses commercial product success to fund research-grade infrastructure, which in turn makes the products more defensible and the mission more credible. The schema is not over-engineering for Babagigi v1. It is right-sized engineering for what Babagigi v1 needs to mean over time.

Where the critic is right: we have not always been clear about this with investors. The pitch needs to include the hybrid model explicitly, including the productivity tax that comes with treating data infrastructure as research-grade rather than minimum-viable. An investor who wants a faster company without that overhead should fund a different company. We need to be willing to say that.

04
You're claiming a multi-generational commitment from a four-person startup.
The critic

The philosophy ends with a commitment to grandchildren in 2080. That is moving rhetoric. It is also, frankly, presumptuous. Most startups die. Most product companies pivot away from their founding mission within five years. Most data archives degrade, lose institutional support, and become inaccessible within twenty.

What is the actual institutional design that would make a multi-generational commitment more than a promise? What happens to the corpus if TomorrowLabs runs out of money in 2028? Who has fiduciary responsibility for the data in 2045 when none of the founders are involved? Where is the irrevocable trust structure, the perpetual license agreement, the partnership with an institution that has institutional momentum across centuries?

Without that, "multi-generational commitment" is marketing copy. The grandmother who records her stories deserves better than marketing copy.

TomorrowLabs's response

The critic is right that the multi-generational commitment, as currently written, is aspirational rather than structurally durable. We have not yet built the institutional design that would make the promise enforceable across decades.

What we will do in response: by end of 2027, every audio corpus contributed to TomorrowLabs will have a designated long-term archive partner outside our control. PARADISEC, ELAR, university linguistics departments, or community-controlled archives, depending on what each community partner chooses. The recordings live in at least two places, one of which is not TomorrowLabs. If TomorrowLabs disappears, the recordings do not.

The harder commitment: we will draft, by Q4 2027, a "sunset clause" — a legally binding document describing what happens to TomorrowLabs's data assets if the organization fails. Default ownership transitions to a named successor institution. Community-contributed data reverts to community ownership. Licenses are pre-negotiated with archive partners. This is the closest a four-person startup can come to multi-generational durability and we have not yet done the legal work.

What we cannot promise: that we will not fail. Most organizations like ours do. The honest version of the commitment is: we are building so that our failure does not also destroy what was entrusted to us. That is different from promising perpetuity. It is more truthful, and the philosophy should say so.

05
"Confidence as first-class data" doesn't fix bias. It just labels it.
The critic

You wrote that every fact carries a confidence level. Useful. But confidence labels do not address the deeper problem: which facts even make it into your database in the first place. Your seed data is heavily skewed toward US diaspora languages where census data is abundant. The languages with the worst data are also the languages with the most urgent transmission risk, and they will appear in your database with "estimated" tags or not at all.

Confidence levels make you feel better about the gaps. They do not close them. And worse, the gaps are not random — they correlate with the languages you should be most attentive to. The Mixtec varieties spoken by a few thousand people each in Oaxacan villages will have no census coverage, no published vitality assessment, no Common Voice data. Your database will know more about Italian than about the languages that need it most.

That is not just a data quality issue. It is a values issue. Your infrastructure will quietly reproduce the inequalities of the academic and governmental data ecosystem you are inheriting from.

TomorrowLabs's response

This is correct and it is one of the things that wakes me up at three in the morning. The data infrastructure cannot be neutral about which languages it knows well, because the world we are inheriting is not neutral.

Two responses:

First, the database design intentionally surfaces gaps rather than hiding them. The data_coverage_gaps view we built explicitly flags languages where speaker data, tech data, or cultural data is missing or stale. A funder or researcher querying our database can see immediately that we know less about K'iche' than about French, and that the relative knowledge gap is itself a research output. We are not hiding the asymmetry. We are making it visible.

Second, the field partnership work is the corrective mechanism, not the database itself. The Cambodia, Guatemala, and Mexico deployments are explicitly designed to generate data for languages the global data ecosystem under-serves. If those deployments succeed, our database will become an unusually strong source for some Wave 4 languages — precisely because we will have collected the data ourselves, with community consent, that no one else collected.

Where the critic is still right: until those partnerships produce data, our database will be lopsided. We need to be careful not to draw confident conclusions from lopsided data. The philosophy should say this explicitly, and currently does not. The next revision will include a commitment to explicit "epistemic asymmetry warnings" on any comparative claim across languages where the data quality differs substantially between them.

06
Where is the community in the writing of this document?
The critic

This is a philosophy document about communities, written entirely by TomorrowLabs, for an audience that is mostly other Western organizations — funders, board members, future hires. The communities whose languages you propose to serve are referenced but absent. They did not draft this. They did not review it. They cannot edit it.

The document does the thing it claims to be against. It uses the voice of institutional authority to make commitments on behalf of communities who were not in the room. Even the most well-intentioned version of this is a colonial gesture in slow motion.

What would it look like for community partners to actually co-author the philosophy? Not just review a draft, but shape what gets said? You do not have to have done this yet, but you should be willing to say that the current document is provisional precisely because they were not part of writing it.

TomorrowLabs's response

The critic is right and this critique is the one that lands hardest. The philosophy as drafted has the structural problem the critic names: it speaks about community partnership in the voice of the partner who already holds the institutional power. The document is currently a TomorrowLabs document about what TomorrowLabs commits to. That is not the same as a partnership document about what communities and TomorrowLabs commit to together.

What we will do: the v1 of the philosophy will be explicitly labeled provisional. Within twelve months, we will draft v2 with active co-authorship from at least one representative from each of our three primary partner communities (Cambodia, Guatemala, Mexico). The drafting process itself is the work, not just the output. Communities will have edit access to the document, not comment access.

What we will commit to as a permanent practice: every major TomorrowLabs strategic document — the philosophy, the partnership agreements, the benefit-sharing framework — will go through community review before it is considered final. Not consultation, which is a one-way information flow. Review, which means communities can require changes and the document does not ship without their assent.

What we cannot promise: that this will produce one coherent voice. Different communities will want different things. The document will get more complicated. That is the right kind of complication. The simpler version we wrote first reflects who was in the room, not what is true.

"You wrote a philosophy because it makes the work easier to fundraise, not because it changed the work."

This is the critique that the others sit on top of. Every one of the responses above commits TomorrowLabs to additional work, additional governance, additional accountability. None of that work is finished. The document presents commitments as if they were positions.

A skeptic could read this entire pressure-test and say: fine, you've added six more aspirational commitments to cover six gaps in the original philosophy. What changed about how you operate today? The criticism would be that the very practice of writing pressure-tests is itself a way of looking sophisticated about one's own shortcomings without actually fixing them.

The honest answer is that some of what this document commits to will get done, and some won't. The benefit-sharing formula probably will, because partners will require it. The community co-authorship of v2 probably will, because we will be embarrassed if it doesn't. The sunset clause and irrevocable archive partnerships are harder, slower, and less visible — they may slip.

The way to know whether this is real or theater is to look in eighteen months at which of these commitments got done, which were quietly dropped, and which were renegotiated in our own favor. Not what we wrote. What we finished. We are going to print this critique alongside the philosophy and revisit it annually.

If the answer in eighteen months is "we wrote the document and then operated as if we hadn't," the critic was right and we should be told so.

What this critique changes

Updates to the philosophy, owed by Q1 2027.

i.
Mark the philosophy v1 as provisional pending community co-authorship. Update the header. Add a note about who was and was not in the room when it was drafted.
ii.
Add a section on operational mechanisms. Each belief needs a paragraph describing how it actually changes what TomorrowLabs products do, not just what the database records.
iii.
Add a benefit-sharing commitment with a deadline. Q2 2027: published framework with specific percentages, applied per language, audited annually.
iv.
Add an institutional durability section. The grandchild-in-2080 paragraph needs to be replaced with concrete commitments to external archive partners and a sunset clause.
v.
Add an "epistemic asymmetry" caveat to any comparative claim. When TomorrowLabs publishes data comparing languages with materially different data quality, the comparison is annotated with the asymmetry.
vi.
Publish this critique alongside the philosophy. Not as an appendix that signals humility. As a structural commitment that the philosophy is incomplete and we are accountable for what we said we would fix.

The point of this exercise is not to be impressive.

The point is to put the critique into our own hands before it lands in someone else's. Every one of these challenges will arrive eventually — from a funder, a partner, a community member, a researcher reading our work. Better that we have already wrestled with it than that we have to learn the answer in real time.

The philosophy is not weakened by this pressure-test. It is sharpened. The version of TomorrowLabs that pretends none of these critiques apply is a version that will eventually be embarrassed publicly. The version that takes them seriously is the version that earns the trust the work actually requires.

We are choosing the second version. The next eighteen months will show whether we meant it.
