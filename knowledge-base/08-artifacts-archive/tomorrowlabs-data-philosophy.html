Data Philosophy · v1

How we treat language as data.

A short articulation of what TomorrowLabs believes about the data we collect, the people whose languages it represents, and the responsibility that comes with holding both.

The Premise

Languages are living infrastructure. So is the data about them.

Languages are not abstractions. They are the way grandparents tell grandchildren who they are. They are the texture of a Saturday morning at a Khmer temple in Long Beach, of a K'iche' market in Sololá, of a phone call between a Cambodian mother in Portland and her sister in Phnom Penh. To preserve a language is to preserve the possibility of those moments — and to document a language is to record evidence of them.

That dual nature — language as lived practice and language as recorded data — sits at the center of what TomorrowLabs does. We build products that help families and communities sustain the lived practice. We maintain infrastructure that treats the recorded data with the care it deserves. These are not two missions. They are one mission, viewed from two distances.

What We Believe

Seven commitments about the data layer.

01
Languages exist in hierarchies, not lists.
A flat catalog of languages flattens reality. Mandarin and Cantonese are not the same kind of thing as their shared label "Chinese." Mixtec is not one language. We model languages at the granularity that matters — macrolanguage, language, variety, dialect — so the data can hold what's actually true.
02
Confidence is a first-class data type.
Every fact we record carries an explicit confidence level: high, medium, low, or estimated. A row marked "estimated" is more honest — and more useful — than no row at all. Confidence makes our gaps visible. Hiding gaps is how research-grade data becomes vapor.
03
Every fact resolves to a source.
We do not hold unsourced data. Every population figure, every endangerment classification, every transmission assessment carries its citation — and the citation carries its reliability rating. If we cannot defend a fact in a footnote, we treat it as a question, not an answer.
04
We use Glottolog as a tool, not as truth.
Academic linguistics gives us a structural backbone that lets our work interoperate with the broader research ecosystem. We use it gratefully. But when community classifications differ from academic ones — and they sometimes will — we record both, and the schema does not flatten the disagreement. Communities have the right to define what their language is.
05
Time-series data tells the truth that snapshots cannot.
A single speaker count is a number. Five speaker counts over a decade is a story — about whether a language is recovering, holding, or being lost. We build time-series into the architecture from day one, because the trajectory matters at least as much as the current state, and because evidence of impact requires baselines.
06
The archive outlasts the organization.
TomorrowLabs as a corporate entity will not exist in fifty years. The corpus we build — recordings, classifications, documentation — could. We choose identifier systems, license structures, and data formats with that horizon in mind. The standard isn't useful to us in the near term; it's findable and usable by a grandchild in 2080.
07
Community-contributed data belongs to the community.
When a grandparent records a story, the recording is theirs. When a community partner contributes a corpus, the terms of use are co-designed. The platform thesis — that communities generate data that improves models that improves products — only works if benefit-sharing is structural, not aspirational. We encode this in the schema, not just in the contracts.
The discipline of building research-grade data infrastructure is not separate from the mission. It is the mission, operating at a layer that doesn't show up in the product.
The Hard Part

We are choosing a particular epistemic framework.

Anchoring TomorrowLabs to academic structural linguistics — Glottolog, EGIDS, UNESCO frameworks — is not neutral. These traditions have histories. They reflect particular intellectual lineages, mostly European in origin, that have not always served the communities whose languages they classify.

We make this commitment with eyes open. The alternative — building our own ontology — would be worse and more presumptuous. The other alternative — refusing to commit at all — would make the work uninteroperable with everyone doing related work elsewhere. So we choose the imperfect tool, and we build in mechanisms for community override.

When the Mayan K'iche' community partner tells us their classification differs from Glottolog's, the schema records both — and the community position is not subordinate. That structural willingness to hold disagreement is the difference between using Glottolog as a tool and using it as truth. It is not a small distinction. It is the distinction.

In Practice

What this commits us to, operationally.

Our practical commitments

Non-negotiable
i.
Every new language we add carries a Glottocode, validated against canonical Glottolog data, before it appears in any TomorrowLabs product or research output.
ii.
We maintain a decision log for every significant classification or roadmap choice, with rationale, owner, and revisit date. Future collaborators inherit our reasoning, not just our conclusions.
iii.
We publish under open licenses — CC-BY for documentation, CC0 where community partners consent — unless community-specific terms require otherwise.
iv.
We contribute back. Field deployments that produce audio corpora contribute to Mozilla Common Voice and equivalent community-owned infrastructure where partners consent. The data flywheel runs in both directions.
v.
We review the philosophy itself annually. The field evolves. Communities push back. New frameworks emerge. A philosophy that doesn't get challenged isn't a philosophy — it's a habit.
In Closing

This is a multi-generational commitment.

We are at the beginning of work that, done well, will outlast us. The data we organize now shapes what is preservable later. The classification decisions we make today affect what a grandchild in 2080 will be able to find of their grandmother's voice.

That weight is not a burden. It is the reason the work is worth doing carefully. The discipline of treating language data with research-grade seriousness is how we honor both the languages and the people who speak them.

This document is itself a commitment — to the reader, to the communities we work with, and to the people who will inherit this organization. We will hold ourselves to it.
