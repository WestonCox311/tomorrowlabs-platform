# Language Data Reference Library
## For TomorrowLabs research and decision-making

Below: 3 links per resource — the best source to learn more, the top YouTube content available, and the most-cited or most-linked external article about it.

A note up front: for several of these resources (PHOIBLE, Grambank, OLAC, CLDF, MultiTree), YouTube content is genuinely thin — these are academic infrastructure projects, not consumer products. Where the YouTube link is weak, I'll note it. Don't take a sparse YouTube presence as a signal of quality; it's a signal of the field.

## PHONOLOGY & STRUCTURAL LINGUISTICS

### PHOIBLE (Phonetics Information Base and Lexicon)
Cross-linguistic phonological inventory database. ~3,000 inventories across ~2,200 languages.

1. **Learn more:** https://phoible.org/
2. **YouTube (sparse):** No definitive talk available. Closest substitute: Steven Moran's University of Zurich faculty page lists his talks — https://www.uzh.ch/cmsssl/en.html — or search "Steven Moran PHOIBLE" on YouTube for lecture recordings.
3. **Most-linked external article:** PHOIBLE Wikipedia entry — https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/PHOIBLE

### Grambank
Largest comparative grammatical database. 2,467 languages, 195 grammatical features.

1. **Learn more:** https://grambank.clld.org/
2. **YouTube:** Hedvig Skirgård (lead author) has multiple talks. Search "Hedvig Skirgård Grambank" — her LingBuzz talks and conference recordings are the strongest content. Also: https://www.youtube.com/results?search_query=Grambank+Max+Planck
3. **Most-linked external article:** ScienceDaily coverage of the launch — https://www.sciencedaily.com/releases/2023/04/230419150234.htm (or the underlying Science Advances paper: https://www.science.org/doi/10.1126/sciadv.adg6175)

### WALS (World Atlas of Language Structures)
Open-access database of 200+ structural features across ~2,600 languages.

1. **Learn more:** https://wals.info/
2. **YouTube (sparse):** No single definitive video. Martin Haspelmath (co-editor) has many lectures on YouTube — search "Martin Haspelmath WALS" for the most relevant content.
3. **Most-linked external article:** WALS Wikipedia entry — https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/World_Atlas_of_Language_Structures

## ENDANGERED LANGUAGES — CATALOGS & FRAMEWORKS

### Endangered Languages Project (ELP) / Catalogue of Endangered Languages (ELCat)
The primary catalog of endangered language data. Maintained by University of Hawaiʻi at Mānoa and First Peoples' Cultural Council.

1. **Learn more:** https://www.endangeredlanguages.com/ (new community-focused site) and https://endangeredlanguages.com/about-catalogue (the catalog itself)
2. **YouTube:** ELP has its own channel — https://www.youtube.com/@EndangeredLanguagesProject — strongest content for understanding the project. Also Google's launch video from 2012: search "Google Endangered Languages Project."
3. **Most-linked external article:** Wikipedia — https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Endangered_Languages_Project (or the related ELCat entry: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Catalogue_of_Endangered_Languages)

### ELAR (Endangered Languages Archive)
Holds digital archives of endangered language documentation. Currently housed at the Berlin-Brandenburg Academy of Sciences.

1. **Learn more:** https://www.elararchive.org/
2. **YouTube:** ELAR has held workshops and posted recordings. Search "ELAR Endangered Languages Archive" or check https://www.youtube.com/results?search_query=ELAR+archive+SOAS. Content is academic but useful.
3. **Most-linked external article:** SOAS departmental writeup of ELAR's history (its original home before moving to Berlin) — https://www.soas.ac.uk/news/elar-celebrates-20-years-funding-language-documentation

### Endangered Languages Documentation Programme (ELDP)
Funds language documentation projects globally. The grant-making arm.

1. **Learn more:** https://www.eldp.net/
2. **YouTube (sparse):** Most ELDP content lives within ELAR's outputs. Their workshop recordings appear on the ELAR/SOAS channels — search "ELDP language documentation."
3. **Most-linked external article:** Their About page provides the cleanest external reference — https://www.eldp.net/about/

### UNESCO Atlas of the World's Languages in Danger
The widely-cited international classification of language endangerment.

1. **Learn more:** https://en.wal.unesco.org/ (UNESCO World Atlas of Languages, the successor project)
2. **YouTube:** UNESCO has produced International Mother Language Day content annually. Search "UNESCO International Mother Language Day" — strongest content for the framework's spirit. Specific atlas video: https://www.youtube.com/results?search_query=UNESCO+Atlas+Languages+Danger
3. **Most-linked external article:** UNESCO Atlas Wikipedia entry — https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/UNESCO_Atlas_of_the_World%27s_Languages_in_Danger

## REGIONAL ARCHIVES

### PARADISEC (Pacific And Regional Archive for Digital Sources in Endangered Cultures)
18,500 hours of audio recordings of small languages and cultures, primarily Pacific/Asian.

1. **Learn more:** https://www.paradisec.org.au/
2. **YouTube:** PARADISEC has its own channel — https://www.youtube.com/@paradisec — with workshop recordings and demos.
3. **Most-linked external article:** Wikipedia — https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/PARADISEC

### First Peoples' Cultural Council (FPCC)
Especially for Indigenous languages of British Columbia and broader First Nations language work.

1. **Learn more:** https://fpcc.ca/
2. **YouTube:** Their official channel — https://www.youtube.com/@FPCCofBC — has language revitalization content and program documentation.
3. **Most-linked external article:** Report of the Status of B.C. First Nations Languages (their flagship publication) — https://fpcc.ca/resource/report-on-the-status-of-bc-first-nations-languages/

## DEMOGRAPHIC & POPULATION DATA

### Migration Policy Institute (MPI)
US and global migration data, including language and diaspora statistics.

1. **Learn more:** https://www.migrationpolicy.org/
2. **YouTube:** Their official channel — https://www.youtube.com/@MigrationPolicyInstitute — has policy briefings and explainers.
3. **Most-linked external article:** Their Migration Data Hub — https://www.migrationpolicy.org/programs/migration-data-hub — is the most-cited single resource from their site.

### IPUMS (Integrated Public Use Microdata Series)
Sample-level census data, US and International. The granular source.

1. **Learn more:** https://www.ipums.org/ (with IPUMS USA at https://usa.ipums.org/usa/ and IPUMS International at https://international.ipums.org/international/)
2. **YouTube:** Their channel — https://www.youtube.com/@ipumsmpc — has data tutorials and webinars. Especially useful: their "Getting Started with IPUMS" series.
3. **Most-linked external article:** Their seminal paper, Ruggles et al., often cited as the definitive source — https://www.nber.org/papers/w22606

### UN DESA International Migration Stock
Global migration data.

1. **Learn more:** https://www.un.org/development/desa/pd/content/international-migrant-stock
2. **YouTube:** UN DESA Population Division channel — https://www.youtube.com/@UNDESAPopulation — has occasional briefings on migration data releases.
3. **Most-linked external article:** Their annual "International Migrant Stock" report PDF, most recent at the time of writing — https://www.un.org/development/desa/pd/sites/www.un.org.development.desa.pd/files/undesa_pd_2025_intlmigstock_2024_key_facts_and_figures_advance-unedited.pdf

### US Census American Community Survey (ACS) — Language Use
Authoritative source for US language data.

1. **Learn more:** https://www.census.gov/topics/population/language-use.html
2. **YouTube:** Census Bureau's official channel — https://www.youtube.com/@uscensusbureau — has explainers on ACS data products. Search "ACS language data" for the most relevant videos.
3. **Most-linked external article:** Their "What Languages Do We Speak in the United States?" feature — https://www.census.gov/library/stories/2022/12/languages-we-speak-in-united-states.html

## SPEECH AI & MULTILINGUAL ML

### Mozilla Common Voice
Open-source voice dataset across 250+ languages, CC0 licensed.

1. **Learn more:** https://commonvoice.mozilla.org/
2. **YouTube:** Mozilla Foundation channel — https://www.youtube.com/@mozilla — has multiple Common Voice videos. Direct search: "Mozilla Common Voice" returns several official explainers.
3. **Most-linked external article:** Their Common Voice Wikipedia entry — https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Common_Voice

### Meta Omnilingual ASR
1,600+ language speech recognition, Apache 2.0, released November 2025.

1. **Learn more:** https://ai.meta.com/research/publications/omnilingual-asr-open-source-multilingual-speech-recognition-for-1600-languages/
2. **YouTube:** Meta AI has covered the release. Search "Meta Omnilingual ASR" — multiple AI explainer channels have covered it. Meta's own AI channel: https://www.youtube.com/@AIatMeta
3. **Most-linked external article:** MarkTechPost's detailed writeup — https://www.marktechpost.com/2025/11/11/meta-ai-releases-omnilingual-asr-a-suite-of-open-source-multilingual-speech-recognition-models-for-1600-languages/ (or the arXiv paper: https://arxiv.org/abs/2511.09690)

### OpenAI Whisper
99-language speech recognition, open-source.

1. **Learn more:** https://github.com/openai/whisper (the canonical project page) or https://openai.com/index/whisper/
2. **YouTube:** Many tutorials. The most-watched cleanest explainer: search "Whisper OpenAI tutorial" — Two Minute Papers and Yannic Kilcher both have widely-viewed coverage.
3. **Most-linked external article:** Their paper "Robust Speech Recognition via Large-Scale Weak Supervision" — https://arxiv.org/abs/2212.04356

### Google Chirp 3 (Universal Speech Model successor)
100+ language ASR, commercial.

1. **Learn more:** https://cloud.google.com/speech-to-text/docs/chirp-model and https://cloud.google.com/text-to-speech/docs/chirp3-hd
2. **YouTube:** Google Cloud Tech channel covers Chirp — https://www.youtube.com/@googlecloudtech. Search "Chirp 3 Google Cloud."
3. **Most-linked external article:** Google's original Chirp announcement blog — https://cloud.google.com/blog/products/ai-machine-learning/bringing-power-large-models-google-clouds-speech-api

### Meta Seamless M4T
Multitask multilingual translation model.

1. **Learn more:** https://ai.meta.com/blog/seamless-m4t/
2. **YouTube:** Meta AI's channel covers Seamless. Search "Seamless M4T Meta" — both Meta's own videos and explainer channels have decent coverage.
3. **Most-linked external article:** TechCrunch coverage — https://techcrunch.com/2023/08/22/metas-new-seamless-m4t-ai-model-can-translate-and-transcribe-close-to-100-languages/ (or the Nature paper for academic depth: https://www.nature.com/articles/s41586-024-08359-z)

### Suno Bark (Generative TTS)
Open-source generative TTS model.

1. **Learn more:** https://github.com/suno-ai/bark
2. **YouTube:** Multiple developer tutorials. Search "Bark TTS Suno" — Matt Wolfe and AI Foundations have widely-viewed walkthroughs.
3. **Most-linked external article:** Hugging Face's Bark integration documentation — https://huggingface.co/docs/transformers/model_doc/bark

## OPEN INFRASTRUCTURE & STANDARDS

### OLAC (Open Language Archives Community)
Federated metadata system across language archives.

1. **Learn more:** http://www.language-archives.org/
2. **YouTube (very sparse):** Almost no dedicated content. Best workaround: Steven Bird (co-founder) has lectures on YouTube relevant to the broader ecosystem — search "Steven Bird language documentation."
3. **Most-linked external article:** OLAC introduction at the LINGUIST List — https://linguistlist.org/olac/

### CLDF (Cross-Linguistic Data Formats)
Standard format for sharing linguistic datasets.

1. **Learn more:** https://cldf.clld.org/
2. **YouTube (sparse):** No dedicated content. Robert Forkel (lead developer) has academic talks — search "Robert Forkel CLDF."
3. **Most-linked external article:** Their published Nature Scientific Data paper — https://www.nature.com/articles/sdata2018205

### OpenSLR (Speech and Language Resources)
Index of open language resources, especially for speech.

1. **Learn more:** https://www.openslr.org/
2. **YouTube (essentially none):** This is a resource repository, not a project with media presence. Workaround: any tutorial on training STT with low-resource languages typically references OpenSLR datasets.
3. **Most-linked external article:** The site itself is the canonical reference; second-most-cited would be the LDC's OpenSLR overview page — https://www.openslr.org/resources.php

### LINGUIST List
The internet hub for academic linguistics.

1. **Learn more:** https://linguistlist.org/
2. **YouTube:** Their channel — https://www.youtube.com/@LinguistListOrg — has conference recordings and explainers.
3. **Most-linked external article:** Their About page — https://linguistlist.org/about/

## A few honest caveats

**The YouTube reality.** Many of these resources don't have strong video presence because they're academic infrastructure projects rather than consumer products. Where I noted "sparse," I'm being honest rather than papering over it. For those, the best path is often to find the lead researcher's name and search their conference talk videos directly.

**"Most-linked-to article" is an inexact phrase.** I interpreted it as "the most-cited external reference," which is usually either a Wikipedia entry, a flagship academic paper, or a major news outlet's coverage of a launch. For obscure resources, this is sometimes just a substantive page on the project's own site.

**Currency check needed every 6-12 months.** Several of these projects have moved hosts (ELAR from SOAS to Berlin, ELDP same path). If a link breaks, search the project name plus "official" — these institutional resources rarely disappear, they just move.

**What to do with this list:** I'd suggest making it a living document inside the TomorrowLabs knowledge base, with a column for "last accessed" and "primary use case for us." That way it doesn't ossify — and the next person who joins TomorrowLabs has a real reference, not just a list.
